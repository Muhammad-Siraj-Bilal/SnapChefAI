NUTRITION BASICS: MACRONUTRIENTS & KEY INGREDIENTS
===================================================

A practical guide to the nutritional profiles of common ingredients, macros, and micronutrients — to help build balanced, informed recipes.

== MACRONUTRIENT OVERVIEW ==

Protein (4 cal/g): Essential for muscle repair, enzymes, immune function. Complete proteins contain all 9 essential amino acids.
Carbohydrates (4 cal/g): Primary energy source. Complex carbs (whole grains, legumes) digest slowly (lower GI). Simple carbs digest fast (spikes blood sugar).
Fats (9 cal/g): Essential for hormones, cell membranes, fat-soluble vitamin absorption (A, D, E, K).
Fibre (0 cal): Feeds gut bacteria, regulates blood sugar and cholesterol. Target: 25–38g per day.

Glycaemic Index (GI):
- Low GI (< 55): Slow glucose release. Beans, most vegetables, whole grains. Best for sustained energy and blood sugar control.
- Medium GI (55–69): Moderate. Whole wheat bread (~71), basmati rice (~58).
- High GI (> 70): Fast release. White bread (~75), white rice (~73), baked potato (~85).

== COOKING'S EFFECT ON NUTRIENTS ==

Heat-sensitive vitamins (partially lost during cooking):
- Vitamin C: Destroyed by heat and water. Steam or eat raw. Water-boiled vegetables lose 50-80%.
- Folate (B9): Sensitive to heat and light. Prefer raw greens when possible.
- Thiamine (B1): Water-soluble; leaches into cooking liquid. Use the cooking water in sauces.

Heat-stable or enhanced by cooking:
- Lycopene (tomatoes): Bioavailability increases significantly when cooked with fat.
- Beta-carotene (carrots): Cooking + fat increases absorption ~6x vs raw.
- Iron (spinach): Cooking reduces oxalates that block iron absorption.
- Sulforaphane (broccoli): Maximise by eating raw or at >3 min boil. Brief steaming is optimal.

Retain nutrients tips:
1. Steam over boil — keeps water-soluble vitamins in the vegetable
2. Minimal water — if boiling, use as little as possible; use the water in soups/gravies
3. Don't over-peel — many vitamins are in or just under the skin
4. Shorter cooking time — the less time above 100°C, the more nutrients remain
5. Eat fat with vegetables — particularly for fat-soluble vitamins A, D, E, K and carotenoids
